What is your educational background?

I am an upperclassman currently pursuing my B.S. of Computer Science Engineering with a Minor in Mathematics at the University of Florida in Gainesville, Florida. Much of my time is devoted to Theta Tau, a professional engineering fraternity. However, you may also find me at Society of Hispanic Engineers (SHPE) meetings, as well as lurking about the dark, dusty halls of the Computer Science building where I also on occasion participate in events with the Programming Team and the Association of Computing Machinery.

Previously, I attended the Honors College at Broward College in Davie, Florida where I successfully obtained my Associates of Arts with Highest Honors. Most of my time spent here was with Phi Theta Kappa, an extremely active honors society on campus, where I served as Vice President of Membership. I often also participated in meetings with the Broward College Math team as a way to broaden my critical thinking and team-building skills.

Why computer science?

I chose computer science because I love waking up every single day and learning something new. There's a bit of a never-ending-story when it comes to this profession. Every day there's somebody working to update the system, so it is imperative to continuouly improve in order to be marketable. I'm constantly looking for ways to reinvent and improve myself. A good rule of thumb that I have is as follows: look at code you wrote a year ago. If you don't say to yourself, "Man, this code sucks!", then you have a problem. Luckily for me, every year I've been able to consistenly say that my code sucks.

How do you use your free time?

When I'm not at school you'll catch me traveling the U.S.A. and attending professional conferences. Some of my most recent attendances include HENAAC and SHPE, where I received many offers for employment, as well as countless interview experiences. Aside from that, I love reading a good book, and am currently working through Skienna's The Algorithm Design Manual.

Ah, I hear it now. "But Jeff" you say, "Shouldn't I be using my all of free time to learn the 107 different types of binary trees for my next technical interview?". Perhaps. Everybody wants to be a better coder and convince themselves that they're as smart as Alan Turing, but nobody wants to be a better professional. Personally, I think that going out of your comfort zone and networking with as many different people as possible is the best way to broaden your horizons in life. Truly, learning the perspectives of others have been instrumental in sharpening my own, and I highly recommend branching out by attending events such as conferences, meetups, and hackathons.
